Conclusion

Many existing platforms offer strong core community features such as posting, discussion, and moderation. Some excel in specific areas: Citizen and Neighbors by Ring focus on real-time, map-based alerts; Reddit and NextDoor enable ongoing conversation and search; and Change.org and Turn Up Activism support cause-driven mobilization. Together, these tools show that awareness and discussion are well-established components of digital community spaces.

While these platforms are useful, the ecosystem is fragmented. This fragmentation forces users to rely on multiple platforms to stay informed, which can hinder participation in their community. There is no single platform that seamlessly connects awareness, verification, communication, and meaningful action. Users often need to switch between tools, and most platforms stop at informing or discussing issues rather than guiding next steps. Young adults, in particular, are underserved, as existing solutions skew toward older audiences or isolated forms of engagement. This presents a clear opportunity to create a centralized, youth-centered platform that bridges discovery, connection, and coordinated action, turning concern into tangible community impact.

Part 2: User Interviews

Who?

Young adults (primarily college students between the ages of 18–25) who are involved in their communities and want to make a positive impact.

How?

Individual, semi-structured interview sessions with an allotted time of 30 minutes.

Method

To better understand our target audience, each team member conducted one 30-minute semi-structured interview with a college student recruited through our extended networks. No identifying information was collected to ensure participant privacy.

We used a prepared question guide with open-ended prompts such as, “How do you stay updated on community events?” and “Do you find it easy or difficult to get involved in causes you care about?” This format allowed for consistency across interviews while giving participants space to share their motivations, frustrations, and experiences. We took detailed notes and captured key quotes to identify patterns in how young adults discover issues, navigate opportunities, and encounter barriers to action. A qualitative approach was selected to better understand the behaviors and perceptions that surveys alone may not reveal.

Part 3: Key Insights

#1: Trust is a prerequisite for action.

Young adults are motivated to support causes they care about, but uncertainty around the credibility and legitimacy of organizations prevents them from fully engaging. As one participant shared,

“There are a lot of organizations that align with what I care about, but I don’t always know if they’re trustworthy.”

Without clear signals of transparency and verification, even interested users hesitate to commit their time or resources.

#2: Friction between awareness and action discourages follow-through.

Participants consistently described caring about local issues but feeling overwhelmed by the fragmented effort required to get involved. One interviewee explained,

“I care, but it feels like I’d have to dig through five different websites just to figure out how to help.”

When engagement requires navigating multiple platforms and unclear next steps, motivation diminishes and participation drops.

#3: Current information channels are inconsistent and untimely.

Most participants rely on social media or word-of-mouth to stay informed, yet they often learn about opportunities too late. As one participant noted,

“I feel like there’s always stuff happening, but I only hear about it after it has already passed.”

Existing systems do not reliably deliver timely, localized updates, limiting young adults’ ability to engage proactively.
